Determine what caused results to deviate from intended goals in the past to better mitigate potential deviations in future rezoning

    • Promoting optimal urban land use is critical to help neighborhoods thrive and make cities sustainable. While market forces encourage the most economic use of land, zoning serves as a check to assure that the community is best served. An implemented rezoning does not always provide the intended results, however, as the Environmental Impact Statement (EIS), the document upon which the rezoning is predicated, sometimes fails to account for iterative developments during the build process. The motivation for this study is to determine what has caused results to deviate from intended goals in past rezonings to better anticipate and mitigate potential deviations in future rezonings.

    This project includes an in-depth case study of the 2004 Downtown Brooklyn rezoning, comparing the goals for the 2013 end of the project’s “build years” (expected time from approval to completion of the project) to the actual results by identifying, collecting and analyzing data from before and after the build.

    Based on the outcome of the Downtown Brooklyn study, we developed an interactive tool which allows a city planner or other user to change the value of various factors which affect the outcome of a rezoning. The predictive tool allows city planners to 1) consider more extreme scenarios and their corresponding effects on the neighborhood; 2) build a mitigation plan for various levels of deviation based on the scenarios; and 3) monitor during the build and set breakpoints at which mitigation must be implemented.
